Virtual Mines, Real Safety: MineXR's Immersive Collaborative Learning for Engineering

December 30, 2025


Figure 1. MineXR integrates immersive virtual environments with mining engineering curricula, enabling students to practice drilling and blasting without real-world risk.

The MineXR project, funded by the Unite! Seed Fund, addresses a critical challenge in mining engineering education: providing safe, practical training for high-risk operations like drilling and blasting. An interdisciplinary consortium of five European universities developed two complementary XR environments: the “VR-Chances” drill&blast simulator and a 360° immersive walkthrough of a Polish granite quarry. Pilot testing at UPC’s Techlab confirmed strong pedagogical impact, with students reporting high engagement and learning value. Building on this success, the consortium now launches MineXR-NXT, a collaborative multi-user platform enabling real-time virtual training across European universities, preparing students for the teamwork essential to modern mining safety.

The Genesis of a Virtual Frontier


Figure 2: Video sample of the VR drill&blast training simulator.

The narrative of the MineXR project begins not in a classroom, but with a stark reminder of human vulnerability within the industrial landscape. Recent incidents underscored a persistent truth: injuries and fatalities resulting from accidents during student practical training remain among the most pressing concerns for universities offering mining engineering degrees. This served as the catalyst for an interdisciplinary team of researchers to unite under the banner of the Unite! Seed Fund for Teaching and Learning. They recognized that, while real-world mining operations demand seamless team coordination for safety, traditional teaching methods were fundamentally limited, unable to safely expose students to collaborative, high-risk environments. Thus, the project was framed with a mission to develop Extended Reality (XR) mine environments specifically for students pursuing Bachelor’s and Master’s degrees, serving as a complementary approach to safety learning.


Figure 3: The 360° Cam App offering an immersive walkthrough of the Piramida-Kostrza granite quarry in Poland.

The aim was clear: to build an innovative, shared open toolkit that would advance mining engineering training across European higher education. This initiative sought to provide students with a safe, immersive environment to conduct practical experiments, thereby enhancing their learning experience and improving their preparation for the complex challenges of the modern mining industry. The coordinating institution, Universitat Politècnica de Catalunya (UPC), worked alongside a consortium of partners including Politecnico di Torino (PoliTo), TU Graz, and Wroclaw University of Science and Technology (WUST), with Khmelnytskyi National University in Ukraine joining as an associated partner. Together, they brought a diverse array of expertise —ranging from mining engineering and computer science to pedagogical design and remote sensing— to bear on the problem of safe engineering education.

Designing the Safety Blueprint

The first phase of the project focused on the critical tasks of planning and design. This stage was not merely administrative; it required a deep dive into the state of the art regarding VR safety training in mining education. The team met monthly via coordination videoconferences to establish timelines, roles, and communication protocols. A pivotal moment in this initial phase was the Needs Assessment. While originally envisioned as a generalist safety report, the consortium made a strategic decision during development to pivot toward mapping specific curricula across the partner universities. This shift allowed the team to identify concrete alignments in content and safety practices, ensuring the final product would be pedagogically grounded and directly useful to the mining departments involved.

A collaborative inquiry identified several critical safety concepts requiring virtual simulation, such as the handling and use of explosives, gas control in underground mines, and the prediction of natural hazards like “rock birds” (rockbursts). These priorities informed the content design, where storyboards were drafted to outline the sequence of events and interactive elements —hazards, safety equipment, and emergency procedures— that would populate the XR environment. The goal was to couple symbolic theoretical information with experiential haptic feedback, ensuring students did not just read about safety, but experienced the consequences of their decisions.

The Architecture of Immersion


Figure 5. A VR overview of the mine where students train drill and blast operations.

Transitioning from design to development saw the digital creation of the XR mines. Led by the technical expertise of GameLabGraz at TU Graz, the team built virtual environments using the Unity engine, incorporating high-quality 3D models, realistic textures, lighting, sound, and animations. A pragmatic choice was made to “piggy-back” on the existing VRChances platform —a tool originally designed to help young adults explore various career paths— and extend it with a straightforward mining scenario focused on surface operations. This approach ensured feasibility within the project’s resources while maintaining consistent aesthetics and stable performance. Development was informed by geophysical characterization of real mining sites to ground the simulations in reality. The result was a training environment focused on the intricate cycle of drilling and blasting, where students assess geological conditions, design drill patterns (calculating burden, spacing, and depth), place interactive explosive charges, and manage complex electrical detonation circuits.

A second environment, led by WroTech and built with the Unreal engine, is a 360° Cam App offering an immersive walkthrough of the Piramida-Kostrza granite quarry in Poland. This application utilizes 18 high-resolution spherical photos and an active information system to provide a first-person perspective on real-world mining operations, bridging the gap between theoretical classroom knowledge and field experience. Both tools were designed for ease of deployment, focusing on standalone VR solutions like the Meta Quest to minimize technical complexity in classroom settings.

Validation at the Techlab


Figure 6. Mining engineering students testing the prototype.

The project reached a significant milestone in November 2025 with a two-day hybrid workshop held at UPC’s Techlab in Manresa. This session served as the primary venue for pilot testing and evaluation, bringing together seven researchers from four countries for live testing alongside students, professors, and members of the press. Participants rated their experience on a scale of 1 to 5, providing critical feedback on usability, comfort, effectiveness, and engagement. The response was overwhelmingly positive, with students and teachers validating the project’s success in enhancing safety skill-building.

Nevertheless, the rigorous testing phase also revealed necessary improvements. Feedback indicated that the hazard map’s visibility needed enhancement and that certain technical details, such as electrical circuit patterns, required correction to better reflect series-circuit reality. For instance, in an electric blaster system, wires cannot cross, and terminology needed to shift from the incorrect use of “fuse” toward more accurate descriptions of series-circuit patterns. Participants also suggested adding more academic content on rock geology and providing clearer visual markers for undrilled holes. This cycle of evaluation and redesign proved essential for continuous improvement, ensuring the XR environment truly achieved its learning objectives.

Impact, Media Reach, and Community Building

The consortium successfully established a digital presence, creating a dedicated repository and online course space on the Unite! Metacampus platform. This space hosts the Open XR toolkit, design documentation, and technical specifications, making these resources accessible to the entire alliance and fostering harmonization of educational practices. The project’s work-in-progress was presented at the II Unite!-Ed Future Conference on March 13, 2025, under the title “MineXR: Building Open XR Mines for Safe Enhanced Mining Education”.


Figure 7. Conference presentation at the 2nd Unite!-Ed Future Conference, Reimagining Education for AI ready graduates.

Public visibility was significantly amplified through targeted press outreach. Notable coverage included an article in La Vanguardia, Spain’s second most-read newspaper. Further features appeared in Regió 7, highlighting the unique virtual simulator designed in Manresa, and official university channels such as UPC News and the Fil Directe bulletin. This level of exposure demonstrated the project’s tangible societal impact and the public fascination with XR technology applied to engineering education. By developing digital twins of mining environments, the project successfully democratized access to remote, expensive, or hazardous locations, addressing an equity gap in mining engineering education across Europe.

Convergence and Collaborative Online International Learning (COIL)

A major outcome of the project’s interdisciplinary synergy was the convergence of teaching methodologies across partner universities. MineXR has transitioned theoretical safety training into hands-on experiential learning, allowing students to acquire practical skills in drilling and blasting without exposure to real-world risks. For early-career researchers and PhD students, the project provided a professional network spanning five countries, fostering relationships that underpin future research proposals and career opportunities. Consortium members noted that the Seed Fund functioned as an ideal incubator, providing essential resources to transform promising ideas into demonstrable prototypes with relatively modest funding.


Figure 8. The team still at work (November 2025).

The initiative has strengthened bonds between mining departments, establishing a consolidated network that serves as the foundation for future technological development. One of the most promising avenues identified is the creation of Collaborative Online International Learning (COIL) formats. The project’s open structure positions it as a springboard for future activities where mining engineering students from different countries can train together in the same virtual environment. This vision of remote collaboration breaks geographical barriers and prepares students for Industry 4.0, where international teamwork and digital agility are paramount.

What Next?

As the initial phase of MineXR concluded with 100% fulfillment of its project objectives, the consortium looked toward the horizon, proposing the “MineXR-NXT” evolution. This next-generation project responds to the critical challenge of preparing students for high-risk operations by transforming the single-user prototype into a collaborative multi-user platform. In MineXR-NXT, students from diverse backgrounds across Unite! universities will simultaneously inhabit the same virtual mine to coordinate drilling and blasting operations in real time. This evolution mirrors real mining teamwork, where safety depends on seamless communication and synchronized actions across borders.


Figure 9. The event aroused the interest of the press, which provided extensive coverage.

The objectives of MineXR-NXT are ambitious: to integrate sophisticated game mechanics, progression systems, and performance analytics to track safety-related behaviors. Adaptive difficulty levels will respond to individual progress, offering remediation or advanced challenges as needed. Furthermore, the project aims to educate on plurilingualism and interculturality, developing culturally agile engineers as recommended by the EU Committee of Ministers. The consortium is actively pursuing funding opportunities to ensure the long-term sustainability and scalability of this robust, openly available tool.

Reflections on the Interdisciplinary Journey

The success of MineXR was not without its challenges. A central difficulty lay in uniting experts from fundamentally different fields —mining engineers concerned with operational accuracy, computer scientists focused on technical implementation, and pedagogues dedicated to learning outcomes. Synthesizing these diverse perspectives into a single coherent product required sustained dialogue and a shared vision. Each discipline brought its own language and priorities, yet the institutional trust provided by the Unite! Alliance allowed researchers to bridge disciplinary and geographical divides.

Ultimately, MineXR stands as proof of the Seed Fund model’s success. By requiring multi-partner consortia, the fund naturally brought together complementary expertise that would otherwise remain siloed, creating a highly productive combination. The project has not only enhanced the learning experience for students within a safe environment but also contributed to the advancement of teaching practices in mining engineering. The open architecture of the platform ensures it can be adapted to other engineering branches, such as mechanical, civil, or chemical engineering, multiplying the impact of the initial investment across the entire network. Through this odyssey, the MineXR consortium has demonstrated that by leveraging the power of XR technology, universities can build a safer, more democratic, and more collaborative future for engineering education.
